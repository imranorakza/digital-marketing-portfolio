01 — Philosophy

Google Ads is not a list of campaigns. It's a system.

Most accounts I've inherited over the years were a collection of disconnected campaigns — good keywords, weak landing pages, no tracking discipline, or the opposite. The accounts that actually produce qualified leads are the ones where every stage feeds the next one. That's the belief my entire approach is built on.

I don't just build Google Ads campaigns. I build lead generation systems, with Google Ads as one part of the process.

02 — Framework

The account architecture I build on every engagement

This is the structure I take into every new account, then adapt to the business. Each layer exists to feed clean data and clear intent to the layer below it.

LAYER 01

Account

One account, one source of truth for billing, conversion actions and shared negative keyword lists. Campaign budgets stay campaign-specific — everything below inherits tracking and negatives from this layer.

LAYER 02

Conversion Tracking

GA4 and Google Tag Manager set up, Enhanced Conversions enabled where possible, primary conversions defined, and offline lead quality fed back in where the CRM supports it. This goes in before campaigns launch — Smart Bidding is only as good as the data it's given.

LAYER 03

Campaigns

Few, consolidated campaigns organised by product line and goal — not by every possible segment. Consolidation is what lets Smart Bidding actually learn.

LAYER 04

Theme-Based Ad Groups

Groups of closely related keywords under one theme — one clear buying intent per group, enough volume for the data to mean something.

LAYER 05

Responsive Search Ads & Assets

A wide, genuinely different set of headlines and descriptions per ad group, with light, deliberate pinning — never a rebuilt static ad. Sitelinks, callouts, structured snippets, call and lead form assets sit at account or campaign level so every ad group benefits from them.

LAYER 06

Keywords

Start on Phrase Match for control, then let proven, profitable search terms graduate to Broad Match once Smart Bidding has data to work with.

LAYER 07

Dedicated Landing Pages

Where it makes sense, every product line gets its own page — its own message, proof and form, rather than a campaign pointing at a generic homepage.

LAYER 08

Optimization

A recurring loop across search terms, negatives, ad assets, landing pages and bids — not a one-time setup.

LAYER 09

Qualified Leads

The business outcome that matters most. Everything above this layer exists to improve both the quantity and quality of those leads.

04 — Campaign Structure

Fewer, stronger campaigns outperform dozens of small ones

Google's own guidance and my own account audits generally agree on this: campaigns split too finely usually don't give Smart Bidding enough conversion data to work with. Every campaign, budget change or bid strategy switch resets the learning phase, and split campaigns often don't collect enough conversions to get through it. Consolidation isn't a shortcut. Where appropriate, it gives Smart Bidding enough conversion data to optimise.

As a general benchmark, I look for campaigns that can reach something like 30+ conversions a month where the budget allows it. Below that, Smart Bidding has less conversion data to learn from. Above it, it has enough data to learn from.

Separate campaigns when —

  • Brand vs Non-brandBranded traffic has near-guaranteed intent and a different bid logic — it shouldn't compete for budget with cold demand.
  • CountriesDifferent markets mean different budgets, languages and often different currencies and reporting needs.
  • LanguagesAd copy and landing pages can't be shared across languages, so the campaigns that hold them shouldn't be either.
  • Major product categoriesERP, Property Management and CRM are different buyers with different budgets — they typically need separate reporting and separate scaling decisions.
  • Search vs Performance MaxDifferent inventory, different signals, different levers — mixing them in one campaign hides which one is doing the work.
  • Lead generation vs EcommerceCompletely different conversion actions and value models — they shouldn't share one bid strategy.

Don't separate campaigns for —

  • DevicesSmart Bidding already adjusts for device signals in real time — a separate campaign per device just fragments your data.
  • Keyword match typesSplitting Phrase and Broad into different campaigns was a control tactic from the manual-bidding era. It now just halves your conversion data.
  • Minor keyword variations"Cloud ERP software" and "cloud ERP system" belong in the same ad group, not their own campaigns.
  • Tiny budgetsA campaign that can't fund 30+ conversions a month will never leave the learning phase — merge it into something that can.
  • Too many campaigns generallyEvery extra campaign is one more place your own campaigns compete against each other in the same auction.

06 — Ad Groups

Theme-based ad groups, not SKAGs

Single Keyword Ad Groups made sense when bids were set manually, keyword by keyword. That approach is no longer recommended for most Search accounts using Smart Bidding. Splitting an account into hundreds of one-keyword ad groups just spreads the same conversion data thinner — and Smart Bidding, and RSAs, both generally need some volume to learn from. In this account, I built each campaign around a small number of themed ad groups instead — for ERP Software, that's just two.

It's not a fixed number — some product lines genuinely justify three or four themed ad groups instead of two. The balance I'm looking for is the same either way: message relevance against the conversion volume Smart Bidding needs to work with.
07 — Keyword Strategy

Start narrow, expand once the data proves it

I normally start with Phrase Match. I usually begin with 5–15 tightly themed keywords per ad group, depending on search intent, budget and search demand.

As the account collects data, I remove poor-performing keywords and add new high-intent search terms from the Search Terms report. Once the account has enough conversion data, I expand into Broad Match with Smart Bidding to scale performance.

→
Prove profitable search terms
→
Broad Match + Smart Bidding

This sequence generally balances control early on with scalability later — I'm not handing the algorithm a blank cheque before the account has enough conversion data. Weekly search term review stays non-negotiable once Broad Match is live, so anything irrelevant gets added to the shared negative list before it burns budget.

08 — Bidding Strategy

Let the account earn its way into automation

I start every new campaign on Maximize Conversions and let Google collect real conversion data without forcing a target on it too early. Once a campaign has built up roughly 30+ quality conversions and performance has stabilised, I'll consider moving it to Target CPA — but only if the business goals actually call for tighter cost control at that stage. There's no universal rule here. Every account, every budget and every sales cycle is different, and I make that call based on what the data shows, not a template.

→

→
30+ quality conversions, stable performance
→
Target CPA, if goals support it

For a change big enough to matter — a bid strategy switch, a major RSA rewrite, a new landing page — I run it as a Google Ads experiment against a control split rather than switching the whole campaign cold. It's slower, but it means I'm making the change because the data supports it, not because I assumed it would help.

09 — Ad Copy

Responsive Search Ads, built to actually get tested

Google gives you up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions per RSA, and the biggest mistake I still see is advertisers writing 15 versions of the same sentence. Every headline needs to stand on its own — Google dynamically combines assets, so any two might end up shown together.

ASSETS

Use the full asset set

15 unique headlines and all 4 descriptions, each highlighting a genuinely different angle — keyword, benefit, proof, offer, differentiator.

PINNING

Pin sparingly, never solo

When I do pin, I pin 2–3 unique variants to a position, never one — a single pinned line just rebuilds a static ad and stops the testing.

AD STRENGTH

A floor, not a target

I aim for "Good" or "Excellent" as a completeness check, then judge the ad on CTR, conversion rate and cost per lead — not the score itself.

What I actually do: I include at least one clear CTA headline and one CTA description in every RSA, and leave the rest of the asset mix flexible so Google's machine learning has room to optimise the combinations around it.
EXTENSIONS

Sitelinks, callouts, structured snippets

Built once at account or campaign level and reused everywhere — extra real estate on the SERP, a direct input into Ad Rank, and extra room the headline didn't have.

LEAD-GEN ASSETS

Call and lead form assets

For B2B accounts where a phone call or a short form is a valid conversion path in its own right, call and lead form assets sit alongside the RSA rather than replacing the dedicated landing page.

11 — Message Alignment

The winning headline becomes the whole message

This is one of the things I do differently. When an RSA headline consistently wins in Google Ads, I generally don't let that insight sit inside the ad account. Depending on the account, it can become the landing page headline, the meta title, the meta description, part of the page content, and often an FAQ answer — carrying the same message across Google Ads, the landing page and the wider website.

Why consistency matters

A visitor who sees the same promise in the ad, the headline and the page copy generally trusts the page faster — that tends to show up in CTR, Quality Score and landing page experience.

Where it compounds

Reusing a proven message across FAQs and supporting content helps reinforce topical relevance and keeps messaging consistent between paid and organic search. It's not a shortcut to rankings — Google Ads doesn't influence organic position — but it does mean the message stays the same wherever a visitor or a search engine finds it.

12 — SEO + Google Ads

Paid search and SEO feed each other, not compete

I treat Google Ads and SEO as one system rather than two departments. Google Ads quickly shows which messages generate clicks and qualified leads. SEO takes that proof and builds long-term, unpaid visibility around it — and what SEO learns about search behaviour feeds straight back into the next round of ad copy and keyword targeting.

STEP 1

Ads prove the message

Winning Google Ads headlines become website content once they've proven themselves against real buyers.

STEP 2

Landing pages support SEO

Dedicated, well-structured product pages built for Google Ads also became strong organic pages in their own right.

STEP 3

SEO improves Ads

Organic search term and ranking data feeds new keyword and messaging ideas back into the paid account.

Using this loop, I helped achieve page-1 rankings for our most important ERP software keywords, while the ad account kept improving in parallel.

13 — Trust Optimization

Conversions go up when confidence goes up first

A qualified visitor still won't fill out a form if the page doesn't look credible. I treat trust as a conversion lever, not a design afterthought — industry logos, customer and executive testimonials, Google reviews, fast loading pages, simple forms and clear security signals all sit on the landing page for a reason.
